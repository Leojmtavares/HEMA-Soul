Historical European Martial Arts
(HEMA)
Refers to the study and practice of combat techniques from Europe, based on historical texts, weaponry, and traditions. Spanning from the Middle Ages to the early modern period, HEMA covers a variety of fighting styles using swords, polearms, daggers, and even unarmed techniques.
Origins
The resurgence of HEMA began in the late 20th century, fueled by the rediscovery of historical manuscripts and a growing interest in reconstructing forgotten martial traditions. Today, HEMA is practiced worldwide through clubs, workshops, and tournaments. Practitioners use protective gear and steel or synthetic weapons to train and spar safely while striving for authenticity.
Modern Practice
HEMA derives its knowledge from surviving manuals, often called fechtbücher (fight books), written by master combatants between the 14th and 17th centuries. Famous examples include Johannes Liechtenauer's longsword traditions, Fiore dei Liberi's Italian school of fencing, and George Silver's English methods. These texts provide detailed instructions on footwork, guards, attacks, and counters.
Weapons and Techniques
Longsword
The iconic weapon of HEMA, practiced extensively due to its rich documentation. Techniques focus on cutting, thrusting, grappling, and timing.
Rapier
A thrusting sword of the Renaissance period, emphasizing dueling and civilian defense.
Sword
Combining a one-handed sword with a small shield, this style was popular in the late medieval era.
Polearm
Such as halberds and spears, used for battlefield techniques.
Dagger
Manuals also cover close-combat techniques, blending weapon use with grappling and throws.
Philosophical and Cultural Value
HEMA is more than just combat. It connects practitioners to the heritage of European martial traditions, fostering an appreciation for historical context, chivalric values, and the evolution of warfare and personal defense.
